Overview

Desertification affects over 100 countries, causing crop failures, poverty, and famine. The issue is worsening, reducing biodiversity and creating a vicious cycle of land degradation. Tree planting is a solution, with efforts like the UNCCD's Great Green Wall Initiative aiming to restore land. Improving soil water retention can enhance vegetation survival. Our team plans to use a bacteria called Komagatataeibactor xylinus DSM 6513 for this purpose, offering new possibilities for desert restoration.

SDG #3

SDG Summary

The United Nations has set multiple goals to achieve good health and well-being for all, with the most important being to ensure healthy lifestyles and reduce the incidence of diseases. Bacterial cellulose (BC) is a product that can effectively reduce water loss from the soil. Our team will improve the genes of Komagataeibacter xylinus to enable it to produce more cellulose, thereby reducing soil water loss, lowering the cost of tree planting in deserts, reducing water waste, mitigating air pollution, and decreasing the probability of people getting diseases. This will create more possibilities and opportunities for increasing the total vegetation in deserts.

Problem Identifications Problem#1 - Intensification of Natural Disasters:

Land desertification leads to the intensification of natural disasters, with the frequent occurrence of sandstorms being the most evident manifestation. During the process of desertification, the reduction of surface vegetation and the decline in soil consolidation capacity make sand and dust more prone to being blown up by the wind. This not only affects the local ecosystem but also has the potential to cause severe impacts on surrounding areas and even further regions. Frequent sandstorms not only damage crops but may also disrupt transportation and people's daily lives. Additionally, sandstorms can trigger other natural disasters such as floods and debris flows, further exacerbating the chain reaction of disasters.

Problem#2 - The Available Land Resources Are Reduced:

Desertification has led to a reduction in available land resources. Since the 1950s, a large amount of arable land, grassland and forest land in China have been converted into sandy land, such as the Houshan area of the Wulanchabu League in Inner Mongolia and the Alxa region, as well as the lower reaches of the Tarim River in Xinjiang. The average degree of desertification in these areas has increased by more than 4%, resulting in thousands of herders becoming "ecological refugees" and being forced to migrate. According to the data of the State Forestry Administration of China, at the end of the 20th century, desertification expanded at a rate of 3,436 square kilometers per year, and every five years, an area equivalent to the size of Beijing lost its use value, affecting a population of 170 million.

Problem#3 - Environmental Pollution:

The impact of land desertification on environmental pollution is mainly reflected in the following aspects: firstly, sandstorms occur frequently in desertified areas, and a large amount of dust is blown into the atmosphere, causing atmospheric pollution. Secondly, during the process of desertification, nutrients and organic matter in the soil are eroded by wind or water, and these substances enter the water body, which may lead to eutrophication or deterioration of water quality. In addition, desertification may also affect regional climate, which in turn affects precipitation distribution and atmospheric stability, indirectly exacerbating environmental pollution. Overall, land desertification not only directly affects the quality of the atmosphere and water bodies, but also indirectly exacerbates environmental pollution by changing the climate.

Solutions to each problem

Solution to Problem#1 - Bacterial Cellulose (BC) Can Significantly Reduce Water Loss:

The reason that BC can prevent water loss is that the OH group and methyl in nanocellulose can form hydrogen bonds with water molecules, and absorb water molecules. Water molecules are fixed on the surface of nanocellulose, so a membrane is formed, which can help to prevent water loss.

Solution to Problem#2 - Bacterial Cellulose (BC) Can Increase Survival Rate of Vegetation:

The bacterial cellulose is a smart biomaterial, an extracellular biopolymer synthesized by several bacteria belonging to the genera Acetobacter, Komagataeibacter, Agrobacterium, Bacillus, and Clostridium Botulin. The nanofibrous network constituting the BC structure consists of well-aligned three-dimensional nanofibers forming a high surface area and porous hydrogel layer. There are many advantages of bacterial cellulose. So BC can prevent water loss to make the vegetation survive easily and can increase the amount of vegetation to increase the available land area. This can help us have more opportunities and possibility for sustainable human development.

Solution to Problem#3 - Bacterial Cellulose (BC) Is a Environmental Friendly Biomaterials:

First, BC can be obtained purely from the culture medium and actively excreted by the cells without any purification process. Secondly, BC naturally has high purity, high crystallinity, high water storage capacity, high degree of polymerization, high surface area, and chemical stability. Which means that BC is capable of increasing water storage at the root part of the plant, as it’s eco-friendly and sustainable. Lastly, compared with other chemical high water storage capacity materials, such as Hydrogel, PAM, and CMC. They are usually expensive and can be broken up into some chemicals which cause leakage and pollution. So BC can reduce the pollution and it can enhance the survival rate of vegetation can further prevent the pollution of sandstorms which caused by land desertification and the pollution of greenhouse gases in the air.

SDG #15

SDG Summary

Terrestrial organisms face multiple challenges, including rapid loss of biodiversity, habitat destruction, environmental pollution, and the impact of climate change. Addressing these issues requires global cooperation and joint efforts, through the development and implementation of effective protection measures, raising public awareness, strengthening scientific research and monitoring, and other means.

Problem Identifications

Problem#1: Loss of biodiversity:

Globally, biodiversity loss is affecting the world, especially in Latin America where species richness has decreased by 94% over the past 40 years, making it one of the most severe regions in the world. This phenomenon reflects changes in land and ocean use, including habitat loss and degradation, which are the biggest threats to biodiversity.

Problem#2: Habitat destruction: :

The threat of land degradation to biodiversity is a serious issue that requires increased investment in biodiversity conservation, the protection of important ecosystems and species, and preserving and restoring habitats for endangered species.

Problem#3: Land degradation:

It refers to the succession process in which the internal structure and physical and chemical properties of the land are altered or destroyed by human or natural factors, or a combination of human and natural factors. As the land environment becomes increasingly harsh, the comprehensive production potential of the land is gradually reduced or lost.

Problem#4: Climate change:

The long-term impact of climate change has not been fully assessed, but it includes issues like global warming and so on. The potential impact on biodiversity cannot be ignored and requires further research and response measures.

Solutions to each problem:

1. Our team focused on using genetically modified Komagataeibacterium xylinus to relieve or even solve the problem of desertification completely. Desertification is a global environmental problem that not only affects land productivity and ecosystem stability but also poses a serious threat to biodiversity. Preventing and controlling desertification is one of the key measures to protect and restore terrestrial ecosystems and promote sustainable use of natural resources, which is why our project can further prevent the loss of biodiversity.
2. By increasing the water holding, the efficiency of replantation will increase, therefore bringing more potential solutions to preventing desertification. Then the number of forests will increase, thereby creating more habitats for animals that rely on trees to survive. For instance, pandas and golden monkeys are endangered animals because of the destruction of their habitats. Protecting threatened species and preventing their extinction is an important component of comprehensive measures to combat desertification.
3. Through preventing and controlling desertification, degraded land and soil, including those affected by desertification, drought, and floods, can be restored, thus striving to establish a world where land degradation no longer occurs. The achievement of this goal is particularly important for protecting mountain ecosystems and their biodiversity, as it helps strengthen the ability of ecosystems to provide essential benefits for sustainable development. ‌
4. Efforts to combat desertification not only contribute to improving the ecological environment but are also key measures to address climate change. By implementing measures such as afforestation and vegetation restoration, the exposed surface area can be effectively reduced, and wind and water erosion can be minimized, thereby slowing down the rate of land degradation. In addition, vegetation restoration can promote soil moisture retention, increase carbon storage, and enhance the carbon sequestration capacity of ecosystems to combat global warming.
